# Night Scenery Through a Train Window: The Gradually Fading Wonderland

Traveling overnight on an ordinary slow-speed train is the perfect union between security and fantasy.

A slow train journey at night brings you through secluded regions rarely witnessed personally, allowing you to savor isolated lands that few have ever laid eyes on. From pure white snow-capped mountains quietly glowing beneath the moonlight, to fairy-tale-like valleys and striking geological wonders, these magical sights feel as though they've stepped directly from a beautifully rendered video game world.

In the past, achieving these untouched views would often demand exhausting treks on foot or long, jolting drives over harsh terrain—enduring severe weather and significant discomfort. Yet today, the cozy sleeper compartments of ordinary trains kindly deliver these hidden wonders right to the comforts of your bed, unlocking scenery you could previously only experience in dreams or imagination.

Traveling by slow trains through the night has become perhaps the gentlest, safest way available today to closely encounter the primitive beauty of our world.

This experience is also uniquely private and exclusive.

In our era of ultra-developed photography technology and the countless volumes of stunning travel images available online, nighttime views glimpsed from slow regional trains remain a special frontier. Current cameras and video equipment simply can't replicate what your eyes instantly capture in fleeting moments—delicate moonlit landscapes, vast panoramas subtly illuminated by stars, nuances of starlight that move alongside your gaze. Today, you can experience such exquisite night visions only with your own eyes. Indeed, the profound emotional resonance created by these visions must be passed on through personal writing, as photographs alone cannot do justice to their ethereal quality.

What makes these night views especially precious to me is their irreplaceable, uniquely Chinese identity. The impressions created by the distinctive sleeper cars of China's "Type 25" traditional trains are heavily reliant on China's immense geographical diversity and a modestly priced fare structure—something largely unchanged for decades. If one day this rare, affordable travel style vanishes from China's railway landscape, I truly don't know where else on Earth travelers could replicate such an accessible yet deeply moving experience—perhaps only through spending a life's fortune on sophisticated luxury tours.

Nighttime slow-speed railway travel holds a special place in my heart, uniquely irreplaceable by any other form of travel. Even if I have to sacrifice every other journey option, I still wouldn't hesitate to choose these nocturnal train voyages. The affordable yet luxuriously unique encounter it provides with scenery is unmatched.

But sadly, I must wonder—just how long will this privileged experience remain accessible?

Modern high-speed railways, with their ultra-fast progress and countless tunnels, are essentially functional transportation tools rather than leisurely scenic journeys. The high-speed railway environment—with intense noise, hurried crowds, bright and intrusive interior lights—makes it nearly impossible to immerse oneself in a quiet fantasy world.

Worse, high-speed services have gradually replaced or squeezed out the existence of classic slow-speed public railway services. These traditional trains, often represented by the beloved "Type 25" sleeper cars, are continually being phased out. Even when older slow-speed trains aren't entirely canceled, they gradually switch to the new CR200J type. Although the CR200J maintains slow speeds, its sleeping cabins no longer boast large panoramic windows. Instead, puny windows prevail, reserved mostly for the lower bunks. Gazing dreamily and expansively upon moonlit valleys, majestic mountain ranges, or endless starfields becomes nearly impossible through these tiny, constraining windows. Furthermore, these newer trains often replace traditional window curtains with built-in blinds, which remain permanently shut once lights are dimmed. While perhaps tidier and cleaner, these blinds reflect unwanted internal lights onto the window panes and heavily diminish the purity of outdoor nighttime sceneries. And worse still—I can no longer quietly appreciate universally enchanting landscapes at night without disturbing fellow travelers.

In other words, little by little, that dreamy nighttime realm beyond the train windows is being steadily compromised and extinguished.

I'll never forget the icy silence of the snow-covered valleys bathed in moonlight along Jitong Railway’s Jingpeng branch line in 2016—an otherworldly beauty that prompted me to fall deeply in love with nocturnal slow-train journeys. Then, in 2018, a nighttime passage through the Kunlun Mountains further deepened my fascination, directly motivating my decision to revisit the Qinghai–Tibet railway again.

Only about five years have passed since these initial unforgettable encounters, and I've had limited opportunities for travel, yet the nightscapes of those train journeys across Qinghai–Tibet railway, Lanzhou–Xinjiang line, Baotou–Lanzhou line, and Jingpeng route have already imprinted themselves deeply in my memory.

I originally planned so much more. Between 2020 and 2021 in particular, I outlined numerous detailed trips—including revisiting certain soon-to-be-shut-down traditional routes such as the older sections of the Jitong railway line. I dreamed of adding a few more surreal memories to my personal archive, imagining how these extraordinary places may have seemed like fairy-tale lands transplanted onto Chinese soil from some distant, magical planet. Unfortunately, due to the pandemic, most of these plans faded into indefinite postponement and regret.

Now, as I reflect on what remains of my cherished nighttime journeys, I cannot help but question how many more magical encounters remain for me before this unique tradition disappears entirely.

How many more fleeting glimpses will I be permitted of this gradually fading railway wonderland?
